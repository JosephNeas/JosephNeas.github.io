Question¶

How does Boulder compare to Colorado, the United States, and other states in terms of their greenhouse emissions?

Hypothesis¶

Global warming has already had a massive impact on the entire globe and continues to do so with the entrapment of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. I think it is important that we have noticed this and have started to take action to combat the rising temperatures. Global warming is a major problem that all of us must be aware of to help change the current trajectory we are headed on. Since the recent attention brought to the problem of global warming since the start of the century, I thought it would be interesting to see how Boulder compares to other places across the US in terms of their emissions released.

For Boulder, I expect to see around the same trend or more of a decrease in emissions compared to Colorado, other states, and the U.S.. I predict that Boulder and Colorado will have relatively the same trend because Boulder is under Colorados government and in the same location. This means Boulder has to have the same restrictions as well as deal generally with the same weather conditions. I expect to see more of a decrease in emissions in Boulder compared to other data points since Boulder is a much smaller being a city. I think this would translate to it being easier to restrict the emissions released allowing for more extreme changes in emissions in a short amount of time. I expect Boulder on a broad scale will follow the trend of the US as a whole because it and Colorado must follow the national laws and restrictions. I also expect to see a negative trend everywhere in the US due to the awareness brought to global warming in recent times and the knowledge of greenhouse gases effect on global warming. I don't expect any major increases in emissions except for maybe an occasional shoot up, but with a continual downward trend.

The graphs in this notebook show overall that there was a general decrease in emissions in Boulder and across the US. The biggest change came in 2010 when the American Clean Energy and Security Act went into effect emphasizing a limitation in the number of emissions each state should output. This overall dropped all emissions except for places like Texas where the increased temperature required a huge demand for energy which translates to more fossil fuels being burned. This is also the reason why most places in the US had an increase in emissions starting in 2016. These temperature changes affected the US as a whole. This means that there was an increased demand for air conditioning and heating in most states. Also during 2016 through the end of the data oil hit a low sales price meaning oil was being bought and used at a much higher rate than it had in the last decade or so.
